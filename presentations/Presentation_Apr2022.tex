\section{Motivation}

\begin{frame}{Motivation}{}
%The post-separation payments are enforced by courts, often specialized labour courts. 

  Courts function poorly in low- and middle-income countries.
  \begin{itemize}
      \item Large backlogs
      \item Long delays in resolution
      \item Parties misinformed
  \end{itemize}. 
  
  \vspace{2mm}
  \noindent There are costs to financial markets (Ponticelli and Alencar (2016)) and the real economy (Boehm and Oberfield (2020)). 
  
  \pause
  \vspace{4mm}
  There is  evidence that court reform can be effective (Chemin, 2020), especially when specialized courts are created (Visaria, 2009). 
  
  \vspace{4mm}
  But much less evidence on how to improve the performance of existing institutions
\begin{itemize}
    \item in particular, few experiments (Greiner and Matthews, 2016)
    \item Root causes of dysfunction
\end{itemize}
  \end{frame}
  
  
 \begin{frame}{Motivation: Long delays}
  The Mexico City Labor Court (MCLC) asked us to help them reduce backlog and increase settlement
  \begin{itemize}
      \item It would take 4 years to deal with backlog even with no new cases
     %\item Besides long  case duration, there is also low settlement rates 
     %\item 90\% of MCLC cases are about firing workers without paying them severance


  \end{itemize} 
  
  \vspace{4mm}
  \pause
  
    With this in mind, we conduct a series of experiments in MCLC cases:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item We provided information on predicted case outcomes to parties in \alert{ongoing} cases, or to workers \alert{prior to filing} a case
        \item Effects on \textbf{settlement rates} and \textbf{plaintiff outcomes} (time, \$, subjective)
    \end{itemize}
\pause
\vspace{4mm}
We take an ``economist as plumber'' (Duflo 2017) approach to asking:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Can we improve outcomes for workers, holding the functioning of the court constant?
    \item Can we learn enough about the causes of the court's problems to help design changes that improve the functioning of the court?
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{What could explain delay?}

With positive surplus, perfect information, common priors, cost of waiting, 2 parties, non-simultaneous offers:  most bargaining models predict \textbf{immediate agreement} (Kennan \& Wislon 1993)

\pause
\vspace{.2in}
Several theories could explain delay in bargaining however
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Overconfidence (Yildiz 2011)
        \item Agency issues, e.g. between the lawyer and his client
        %\item Asymmetric Information (Kennan & Wilson 1993): delay is useful to credibly communicate information
        \item Others (Asymmetric Information, Behavioral biases)
    \end{itemize}
\vspace{.1in}


In the back of our minds we have an overconfidence story (we found that sum of probabilities of winning $>1$)


%\begin{itemize}
%    \item We experimentally provided information
%\end{itemize}

\vspace{.15in}
%We don't control the bargaining game: we will focus on the reduced form effects of giving information.

\pause
\textbf{Overconfidence can narrow the settlement range}: I ask for more in bargaining because I expect to get more at court ruling... but the other side thinks \textit{they} are going to win


\begin{itemize}
    \item A typical prescription of in overconfidence and asymmetric info models is to provide information by a trusted party... which is what we tried to do
\end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Context: low use of courts}

We address cases where workers claim they were fired without cause and did not receive legally-mandated severance payments.

\vspace{.1in}

Mexico's law requires severance payment when a worker gets fired.

\vspace{.2in}
Based on a survey to 2 million formal sector workers we implemented with IMSS, we find that:

\vspace{.1in}
\begin{itemize}
    \item 50\% of fired workers don't receive severance.
    \vspace{.1in}
    \item Of those that do not receive severance, only 10\% sue. 
    \vspace{.1in}
    \item The remaining 90\% say they don't sue because:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item It is useless to sue (30\%)
        \item It is expensive use the court (50\%)
        %\item Don't know any lawyers (26\%)
        %\item Did not know it was possible (23\%)
        
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{.1in}
    %\item Suggests that malfunctioning courts limits access to justice
    \vspace{.1in}
    %\item It is important to understand why they malfunction.
    %\vspace{.1in}

\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Context: Mexico City Labor Court}{}
%The post-separation payments are enforced by courts, often specialized labour courts. 
  The Mexico City Labor Court receives 30,000+ filings a year. Cases are assigned to one of 20 "sub-courts". \\
\pause
 \vspace{.2in}
The majority (90\%) are unfair dismissal cases filed by individual workers suing employers for severance pay. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item about 10\% of cases handled by free public layers.
    \item private lawyers charge fixed fee + 30\% of winnings (+``processing fees'' sometimes)
\end{itemize}

\pause
\vspace{.15in}

We view courts as facilitating bargaining between the plaintiff and defendant. 
   \begin{itemize}
       \item Most cases reached a bargained settlement after suing (55\%). 
       \item However the 55\% settlement rate in the MCLC is lower than rates for similar cases of 79\% in Australia, 80\% in the U.S. 90\% in Sweden, etc.  
       %\item Can act as a trusted mediator and decrease asymmetric information if it helps reveal info. Plus, going to court itself can be a signal that could reveal information. 
   \end{itemize}
 \end{frame}
 
 

 \begin{frame}{Context}
    
The law does not work so well for workers in practice:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Workers often at least partly informal (Kumler et al. (2020))
        \item Employers and lawyers know some tricks (e.g. resignation letter)
        \item Collection of awards is challenging (e.g. no registered assets)
    \end{itemize}

\pause
\vspace{5mm}
Other issues:
\begin{itemize}
        \item Workers inexperienced: $>$85\% first-time users of the court; low schooling/ low income 
        \item Many lawyers are ``coyotes''
        \item Anecdotal evidence of corruption, and of lawyers trying to convince workers with losing cases to sue.
        \item Little information about lawyer quality (harder to accumulate reputation).
    \end{itemize}
    
    \pause
    \vspace{.2in}
    \textbf{Potential misalignment of incentives to settle:} workers vs their private lawyers
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Lawyers charge \$100 usd to start, likely$>$ marginal cost
        \item Lawyers handle many cases (diversified) and potentially more patient than workers (waiting for judge decision is like buying lottery ticket).
        
    \end{itemize}
    

  \end{frame}

Our experiment will provide evidence on three questions: 

\vspace{5mm}
\begin{enumerate}
      \item Does providing information on expected outcomes increase settlement rates? (e.g. by reducing  overconfidence or information asymmetries)

            \item Workers represented by private lawyers are even less informed (controlling for observable characteristics)
        \end{itemize}

      \item The sum of subjective probabilities of winning (plaintiff + defendant) for the same cases is 1.47
      \item Plaintiffs on average think they will win with 78\% probability, while their average win rate in the data is 41\% (37pp difference); for defendants the difference is only 10pp.
      
  \end{itemize}
  
  \pause
  \vspace{5mm}
  \item \textbf{Fact 5: Private lawyers ask for more but get the same (Historical Data)} 
         \vspace{2mm}
      \begin{itemize}
          \item Controlling for case characteristics, private lawyers file claims almost twice larger, but recover the same as public lawyers. 
          \item Plaintiff receives even less after accounting for fees.
          \item Accounting for fees 40\% of those with private lawyers lost money.
      \end{itemize}  
  
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{Design}{}
\vspace{-.5in}
   \textbf{Randomization:} At the case level (Phase 1) or the subcourt-day level (Phase 2), or the day level (Phase 3), we randomly assign parties to one of two conditions:
        
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \textbf{Treatment:} Information from the ``Calculator'' with predicted outcomes based on the characteristics of their case (e.g., wage and tenure). 
            \item \textbf{Control:} business as usual (or very basic information in Phase 3).
        \end{enumerate}
                \vspace{4mm}
      \pause         
     \textbf{Outcomes}
     \begin{itemize}

         \end{itemize}
               
    \end{itemize}

 \begin{frame}{Endogeneity of the presence of the plaintiff, Phases 1 and 2}

\section{Plaintiff welfare effects}
\begin{frame}{A simple framework}
Why might that be?
\vspace{.1in}

    In Appendix B of the paper, there is a simple framework that motivates:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Why settlement might be beneficial for plaintiff
        \item Why the plaintiff and her lawyer may disagree on whether to accept a settlement.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \pause
    \vspace{.1in}
    In the interest of time, I'll just say here that we expect that the plaintiff and their lawyer differ in two important respects:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Lawyers handle many cases simultaneously, and hence can diversify the risk of any single case.
        \item Lawyers have higher incomes and possibly lower discount rates (50\% of workers say they are having problems putting food on the table).
    \end{enumerate}
    \vspace{3mm}
    
    \pause
    
    Both of these will lead lawyers and their clients to differ in the choice between uncertain future payoffs (from judgment) and a certain settlement today.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Lawyers prefer to proceed to judgment when plaintiffs prefer to settle
        \item This provides motivation for withholding the information from clients
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Conceptual framework}

\begin{frame}{Conceptual framework}
Construct a simple framework around:
\begin{itemize}
\item overconfidence of both parties
\item long durations with high discount rates
\item uncertainty over both award and ability to collect. 
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.1in}

Four parties: Plaintiff, plaintiff\’s lawyer, defendant, \textit{defendant\'s lawyer}.
\vspace{0.1in}

Plaintiff hires lawyer to file claim G(X) with expected payoff P ${[0, \overline{P)}}$, made by the defendant to the plaintiff, after case duration D. 
\vspace{0.2in}


Focus first on plaintiff and defendant. Denote plaintiff's expectations as $({D_p, P_p})$ and defendant's expectations as $({D_d, P_d})$.

\vspace{0.1in}
\textbf{Define} $\rho_i$ as the discount rate of player i \in{(p,d,a)} and  $\delta_i$ as the corresponding discount factor.
    
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Conceptual framework}

There is uncertainty over the amount and timing of the award. Ignoring for now that uncertainty, the present values of award / payment are:

       \vspace{0.1in}

 ${\delta_p^{D_p}}{P_p}$ 
       \vspace{0.1in}

and  ${\delta_d^{D_d}}{P_d}$ 
       \vspace{0.1in}

The parties will have an incentive to settle when:
       \vspace{0.1in}

${\delta_p^{D_p}}{P_p} <$  ${\delta_d^{D_d}}{P_d}$.

       \vspace{0.1in}
That is, when the plaintiff discounts the future more than the defendant. \textit{(Evidence coming, but we see this as very reasonable.)}

\vspace{0.1in}
When there is no overconfidence and ${\delta_p}<$ ${\delta_d}$, we should see immediate settlement. \textit{(Note that the lawyer’s discount factor will also matter here.)}
	\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Conceptual framework}

A few issues to highlight:

\begin{itemize}
         \item Overconfidence of the parties can eliminate the contract zone even if plaintiffs have higher discount rates. (\textit{Think calculator treatment)}
\vspace{0.1in}
	\item Settlement may not occur even with a non-empty contract zone. (\textit{Think conciliator treatment)}
\vspace{0.1in}
	\item If plaintiff and/or defendant are risk averse, the contract zone will be larger.
 \end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Contract zone and over-optimism}

    \begin{figure}[H]
    \label{calc_template}

        \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{presentations/ContractZone.pdf}
        \end{center}
\end{figure}

\begin{frame}{Divergence of plaintiff/lawyer incentives}
Finally, consider the potential for a divergence in the incentives of the plaintiff’s attorney and the plaintiff. Focus private attorneys - around 85\% of our cases.

\vspace{0.2in}
Same idea: Bargaining between the plaintiff's lawyer and the defendant. The plaintiff's lawyer typically receives 30\% of any award, with a present value of ${\delta_a^{D_a}}{P_a}$.

\vspace{0.2in}
Ignoring for now the opportunity cost of time, a non-empty contract zone in the game between the plaintiff's attorney and the defendant if: ${\delta_a^{D_a}}{P_a} <$  ${\delta_d^{D_d}}{P_d}$ 

\vspace{0.2in}

The plaintiff will want to settle and her attorney will not if: 

\vspace{0.1in}

${\delta_p^{D_p}}{P_p} <$  ${\delta_d^{D_d}}{P_d} <$ ${\delta_a^{D_a}}{P_a}$  

\vspace{0.1in}
Plausible if: ${\delta_p}<$ ${\delta_d}$\leq${\delta_a}$.

\vspace{0.1in}
	Plaintiff is risk averse and her lawyer is not

\end{frame}



\begin{frame}{Divergence of plaintiff/lawyer incentives}
    More generally, there is a potential divergence in the incentives of the plaintiff and her lawyer.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Different discount factors (\textit{plaintiff discount rate $>$ lawyer discount rate: plaintiff wants to settle when lawyer does not})
    \item Different levels of optimism (\textit{plaintiff more optimistic than lawyer: lawyer wants to settle, plaintiff does not})
    \item Different risk aversion (\textit{plaintiff risk averse, lawyer diversified across cases: plaintiff wants to settle, lawyer does not})
    \item Differences in opportunity cost of time (higher for lawyer (?): \textit{lawyer wants to settle, plaintiff does not})
\vspace{0.1in}

\end{itemize}

The lawyer may also be concerned with reputation effects, so perhaps cannot admit to being less optimistic on outcomes. 


\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Predictions}
Several predictions from this framework:
\vspace{0.1in}
\begin{itemize}

  \item If plaintiffs have higher discount rates than defendants, then an immediate settlement is the efficient outcome.
  
\vspace{0.1in}
    \item If both parties have the same discount rate, risk aversion may still create a non-empty contract zone range.
    
    \begin{itemize}
    \item However, in that case, the timing of the settlement does not affect the level of surplus generated by the settlement.
    
    \end{itemize}
 \vspace{0.1in}
    \item The preferences of the plaintiff's and her attorney may diverge if:
    
\begin{itemize}
\item Degree of overconfidence differs
\item Discount rates differ
\item Risk aversion differs. 

\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.1in}
All three are likely to be true (\textit{data coming}), with the first making attorney more willing to settle and the other two  making the plaintiff more willing to settle.
\vspace{0.1in}
Predictions:
	\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Calculator} Will reduce overconfidence and hence increase the contract zone, to the benefit of both parties
	\item \textbf{Conciliator} Will encourage settlement when there is a non-empty contract zone, but a breakdown in bargaining.
\end{itemize}
 \end{itemize}
\end{frame}



  \vspace{.1 in}
    \item \textbf{The "Calculator":}
        \vspace{0.05in}
            \begin{itemize}
                \item \textbf{Sample:} 2500 \textit{concluded} cases from sub-court 7. Random sample of all cases filed in 2011 and 2012 that were concluded by the end of 2015.
                
                \item \textbf{Variables:} Initial labor suit variables (e.g. wage, tenure); When the suit ended and how (settlement, drop, judgment, or expiry); compensation received by the worker.
            \end{itemize}
            \vspace{0.1in}
\item \textbf{Conciliator}: Each subcourt has one conciliator; for the experiment, the MCLC assigned an additional conciliator to subcourt 7.
